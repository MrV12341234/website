On the glass above the doors in our airbnb are a man and women. We have seen different variations of this male and female all over Sicily. They are a symbol of the island.

There are two separate legends to these figures. Both legends you will hear as you travel across Sicily.

The first goes like this:

An arab man comes to Sicily and they fell in love (she is Sicilian). After they are together for a while, he decides to go back home, leaving Sicily, to return to his family and children. Well the women is so angry that he didn’t tell her he had another family that she killed him and used his head a lot to grow basil.

The second story goes like this:

There were two lovers, one is a Sicilian woman, The other is an Arab man. Their families did not approve of their love, so the families killed the lovers and hung up their heads as a warning to others to not intermix.

2.14.26 Day 6. We walked to the Archeological park of Neapolis, a UNESCO world Heritage site that has preserved the remains of several Roman and Greek structures.

The first site we saw a Greek amphitheater (Teatro Greco). It was built around 500 BC, this is when the town of Syracuse (and Sicily as a whole) was ruled by the Greeks. But when the Roman’s came into power, the remodeled the theatre (and even built their own right next door).

There’s a local Italian company (INDA) that builds a modern wood stage and puts on a show each year, using the original theatre seats! You can see the workers building the stage for this years show.

Just a few minute walk away and we were at the Roman arena. The Greek theatres were a half circle but the Romans built their arenas in an oval shape so guests could have a 360 degree view of the show. And of course, the round arenas made it easier for the animals and gladiators in.

This Roman Amphitheater was built during the early imperial years, so around 200-300 CE.

We sat there and pondered where the gladiators came out and how they stayed inside. And we pondered where all the guests would enter and take their seats. And to wonder how many gladiators died right there in that center oval. And what animals did they battle? Lions? Tigers?

If you notice in the second photo of the Roman arena, one side has significantly less seats still there. This is because, in the 1500’s, when the Spanish ruled Sicily, the city of Syracuse / Ortigia was being turned into a major fortress, so the Spanish (under emperor Charles V) took the stone blocks away from the arena to build a protective wall somewhere in the city. The other side of the arena was carved directly into the mountain, so there were stones to be excavated by the Spanish.

And speaking of these large stone blocks, where did they come from?

Well right behind the Greek amphitheater was a MASSIVE quarry. This quarry was dug not only for the arenas but also to build the town of Ortigia. But what’s interesting is they made these caves when digging instead of just making holes.

This is the entrance of one cave. The cave goes in and then makes a sharp right turn. The next three photos are taken from inside this cave, each picture being deeper into the cave than the last photo.

2.17.26 Day 9

We drive from Ortigia (Syracuse) to Agrigento.

Agrigento is this really cool town that was built by the Greeks and the Roman’s, but later when the Normans arrived (around year 1100), they moved the city up onto the hill behind the town. The town has many temples that were built throughout the years, including the Temple of Hercules. The actual Hercules!

Because of the short opening hours we weren’t able to make it to the “Valley of the temples”, the Unesco world heritage site where all the temples can be seen (by paying lots of money of course).

So we decided to see the “Stairs of the Turks”. This is a unique formation of limestone rock that meets the Mediterranean Sea.

Because it’s off season there were only a few people here and we got some good photos without any tourists. But it was very windy.

The name “Stairs of the Turks” comes from two things. First, and most obvious, the rock formation looks like stairs. And second, pirates during the 16th century would land here and use the formation as easy (and hidden) access to get up on flat ground and raid the coastal towns. The pirates were Saracen and Barbary (Middle East and North African people/pirates). The locals called both these groups, who came to steal goods and capture slaves, “Turks”. Stairs of the Turks.
